In the low marshlands between two rivers, where the reeds grew taller than a man and dragonflies shimmered like pieces of broken glass, there lived a frog named Brindle.

He was not an extraordinary frog to look at. His skin was mottled green and brown like wet bark. One eye was slightly larger than the other. His croak sounded less like the deep music of the pond frogs and more like a rusty hinge.

But Brindle possessed something rare among frogs:

He wondered about things.

Most frogs cared for practical matters—flies, weather, herons, mating season, mud depth, and whether the snapping turtles were active near the cattails. Brindle cared about those things too, but at night he would sit on a stone beside the water and stare upward.

“What are the stars?” he once asked an older frog.

“Cold flies,” the old frog answered.

“They don’t move.”

“Then they are dead flies.”

This explanation satisfied everyone except Brindle.

So he kept wondering.

---

The marsh was called Blackwater Fen, though the water was not black except after storms. It stretched for miles, tangled with willow roots and drifting mats of moss. The creatures there believed the Fen was the entire world.

Brindle suspected otherwise.

At dawn, migrating birds crossed the sky in vast shifting ribbons. Sometimes they called to one another in voices so distant and strange that Brindle felt his heart pull upward.

Places, those birds seemed to say.

There are places beyond this place.

One autumn evening, after a rainstorm silvered the marsh, Brindle met a traveler.

The traveler was a turtle.

Not one of the Fen turtles—those thick, grumpy creatures who buried themselves in mud and disliked conversation. This turtle wore river algae across his shell like green velvet and carried a tiny brass bell tied around his neck with reed-fiber string.

He emerged from the fog without sound.

Brindle nearly leaped into the water.

“Easy,” said the turtle calmly. “I am old, not hungry.”

“You’re not from here,” Brindle said.

“No.”

“Where are you from?”

The turtle smiled slightly.

“A long way away.”

Brindle moved closer.

“How long?”

The turtle considered this.

“Long enough for mountains to become hills.”

Brindle stared.

“You’ve seen mountains?”

“I have.”

“What are they?”

The turtle looked at him carefully then.

Most creatures asked mountains for practical reasons. Can you eat them? Can you hide beneath them? Are there insects there?

But Brindle’s eyes held genuine hunger.

“They are stones,” said the turtle softly, “that touch the sky.”

Brindle forgot to breathe.

---

The turtle’s name was Odo.

He remained in Blackwater Fen for three weeks, resting before continuing southward. During that time Brindle followed him endlessly, asking questions.

What lies beyond the marsh?

Forests.

What lies beyond forests?

Fields.

And beyond fields?

Villages.

And beyond villages?

Cities.

And beyond cities?

The sea.

Brindle could scarcely imagine so much world existing at once.

At night Odo told stories.

He spoke of foxes with white winter coats, enormous bridges built by humans, caves filled with glowing blue insects, and deserts where rain never fell.

“Impossible,” muttered the other frogs.

“True,” said Odo.

Brindle believed him.

One night, while moonlight painted the reeds silver, Brindle finally asked the question that had been growing inside him since Odo arrived.

“How do I see these places?”

The turtle looked toward the south.

“You leave.”

The word struck Brindle like cold water.

Leave?

Frogs did not leave.

Frogs hatched, swam, climbed onto lily pads, survived winters, sang in spring, and eventually vanished into mud and memory.

But leave?

That belonged to birds.

“I’m too small,” Brindle whispered.

Odo chuckled.

“The world is large for everyone.”

---

When Odo departed, the marsh returned to normal.

But Brindle did not.

The old routines now felt like sleepwalking. He hunted flies automatically. He listened to endless croaking contests with growing impatience. Even the moon seemed different now that he knew it shone over mountains and oceans too.

The others noticed.

“You’re strange lately,” said Mire, a young frog who lived near the western reeds.

“I’m thinking.”

“That’s your problem.”

Brindle laughed weakly.

Perhaps it was.

But once a mind stretches beyond its old borders, it rarely shrinks back willingly.

Weeks passed.

Winter approached.

Migrating birds gathered overhead in thunderous clouds.

And one freezing dawn, Brindle made his decision.

He would leave Blackwater Fen.

---

The journey began badly.

Within an hour he nearly died twice.

First, a water snake lunged from beneath a rotten log and missed him by inches. Then a hawk spotted him crossing open ground and forced him into a ditch thick with freezing mud.

By sunset Brindle was exhausted, filthy, hungry, and terrified.

The marsh already lay far behind him.

He sat beneath a mushroom and wondered if he had made the greatest mistake in frog history.

Then he looked up.

The stars.

Not dead flies.

Not lanterns.

Not holes in the sky.

Something vast and ancient and unreachable.

Brindle straightened.

And continued.

---

Beyond Blackwater Fen lay grasslands.

To Brindle they seemed impossibly enormous. Wind rolled through them in waves. Strange flowers bent under the weight of bees larger than his head.

He met creatures unlike any in the marsh.

A field mouse who spoke so quickly Brindle could barely understand him.

A snail philosopher who believed motion was an illusion.

A pair of quarrelsome crows who tried unsuccessfully to convince Brindle to become a “fortune-telling swamp prophet” for profit.

For the first time in his life, Brindle realized every creature believed its own way of living was normal.

The mice thought frogs were gloomy.

The crows thought frogs were humorless.

The foxes thought frogs tasted excellent.

The world, Brindle discovered, was filled with opinions.

---

Months passed.

Brindle crossed streams and roads and forests blazing red with autumn leaves.

He saw human towns from hidden ditches.

At night windows glowed gold against darkness. Music drifted through the air. Dogs barked. Children laughed.

Humans fascinated him.

They built nests from stone.

They carried fire indoors.

They walked everywhere on only two legs and somehow did not constantly fall over.

Once Brindle sneaked into a garden pond behind a bakery.

The smell nearly drove him insane.

Warm bread.

Butter.

Sugar.

He remained there for three glorious days until a cat arrived.

Then he departed immediately.

---

Winter found him in the northern woods.

Snow terrified Brindle.

The first flakes fell silently through pine branches while he watched from beneath a fern.

Cold white ash from the sky.

By morning the world had vanished.

Everything was buried.

Brindle survived only because he encountered an ancient toad named Agnes who lived beneath tree roots beside a frozen creek.

“You’ll die out there,” she grunted.

“I know.”

“Then stop doing that.”

Agnes allowed Brindle to shelter with her through winter.

She was ancient beyond measure, scarred by owls and snakes and time itself. One toe was missing. Her left eye clouded white.

She also possessed the sharpest mind Brindle had ever encountered.

“You ask too many questions,” she told him.

“I’ve heard that before.”

“Good. Means you’re consistent.”

During long winter nights Agnes taught him things.

How to predict weather from wind scent.

How humans poisoned rivers without meaning to.

How silence could reveal danger faster than sound.

How fear and wisdom were not enemies.

Most importantly, she taught him this:

“The world is bigger than understanding. Don’t shrink it just because uncertainty frightens you.”

Brindle never forgot those words.

---

When spring finally returned, he continued east.

And eventually, after nearly two years of wandering, Brindle reached the sea.

At first he thought it was another sky.

Endless blue stretching beyond sight.

Waves thundered against rocks with the voice of giants.

Brindle stood motionless for nearly an hour.

All his life he had imagined the sea.

Reality was larger.

The air tasted alive.

Birds wheeled overhead screaming into the wind.

The horizon curved away forever.

Brindle laughed aloud.

A tiny frog laugh against an infinite ocean.

And yet somehow it mattered.

---

He lived by the coast that summer.

He learned tides.

He learned the moon controlled more than light.

He learned crabs were rude in every conceivable language.

One evening he climbed a cliff during sunset and looked westward over burning orange water.

For the first time since leaving Blackwater Fen, he felt something unexpected.

Homesickness.

Not for the mud.

Not for the insects.

Not even for safety.

For familiarity.

For voices he understood without effort.

For the old stone beside the water where he used to watch stars.

Brindle realized then that travel changes a creature in two ways.

It expands the world.

And it transforms home into something impossible to fully return to.

---

Years passed.

Brindle grew older.

Scars marked his legs.

One winter frost permanently stiffened his right foot.

He saw cities.

Mountains.

A desert that glowed red at dawn.

He crossed rivers wider than Blackwater Fen itself.

And everywhere he traveled, he listened.

Stories from beetles.

Warnings from owls.

Songs from whales heard faintly through harbor waters at night.

The world was endlessly stranger than he had imagined.

And endlessly more beautiful.

---

At last, when Brindle had become an old frog himself, he returned to Blackwater Fen.

The marsh looked smaller.

Not lesser.

Just smaller.

Young frogs crowded around him almost immediately.

“Is it true there are rivers wider than the marsh?”

“Yes.”

“Are there really stones taller than clouds?”

“Yes.”

“Is the sea real?”

Brindle smiled.

“Yes.”

They stared at him with wide golden eyes.

Some disbelieved him.

Some mocked him.

But a few listened with dangerous attention—the kind that leads creatures away from safe places.

Brindle recognized that look instantly.

One evening a tiny frog approached him beside the old stone where he once watched stars.

“Were you scared?” she asked.

“All the time.”

“Then why leave?”

Brindle looked upward.

The stars shimmered over Blackwater Fen exactly as they always had.

Because the stars had never changed.

Only he had.

“Because,” he said softly, “wonder is difficult to ignore.”

The young frog sat beside him silently.

And together they watched the enormous sky.
